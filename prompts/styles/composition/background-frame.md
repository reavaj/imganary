# Background Frame

An image designed to serve as a backdrop for a composited portrait or subject. Visual interest supports and frames without overpowering — a stage waiting for its actor.

## Visual DNA
- **What it does:** Creates an environment that frames and supports a future subject without competing for attention. The composition provides visual interest while leaving room for a subject to be composited on top.
- **Key characteristics:**
  - No human figures, faces, or dominant standalone objects that would compete with a composited subject
  - At least one area of relative calm or lower detail where a subject could naturally sit — this does NOT need to be centered; off-center, rule-of-thirds, or asymmetrical placements are encouraged
  - Visual weight can be distributed asymmetrically — heavier on one side, top-heavy, bottom-heavy, or diagonal
  - Framing devices can be partial — elements on one side or two sides rather than a full border
  - Detail and texture variation across the image, with at least some breathing room
  - Depth cues that create a sense of "behind" — the background should feel like it recedes, not compete on the same plane
  - Tonal harmony — nothing so bright or saturated that it would clash with an overlaid subject
  - Natural framing devices: arches, foliage, architectural borders, radiating patterns, concentric shapes, diagonal elements, asymmetrical clusters
- **Reference points:** Album cover backgrounds, movie poster compositions, theatrical stage backdrops, portrait studio environments, title card backgrounds, magazine editorial layouts
- **Pairs well with:** Any style — this is a compositional modifier, not a visual style. Combine with a style (e.g., cyberpunk, watercolor, art deco) for the actual aesthetic.
