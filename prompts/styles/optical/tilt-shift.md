# Tilt-Shift / Miniature

The miniature effect — real-world scenes rendered with extreme shallow depth of field that makes cities, landscapes, and crowds look like tiny scale models.

## Visual DNA
- **Lighting:** Bright, even daylight works best (overcast or midday sun). The effect is strongest when lighting is flat and even — shadows that are too dramatic break the miniature illusion. Saturated, almost toy-like color rendering.
- **Composition:** High angle or aerial looking down at a 30-60 degree angle. The viewing angle of someone looking down at a model railway or diorama. Scenes with recognizable scale cues — vehicles, people, buildings — that get recontextualized as tiny. A narrow band of sharp focus with everything above and below dissolved into creamy blur.
- **Color palette:** Slightly oversaturated, toy-like colors. Primary-colored vehicles and buildings pop against neutral grey roads and green grass. The clean, bright palette of a well-maintained scale model. Think Wes Anderson meets Google Earth.
- **Textures & materials:** The sharp-focus band reveals real-world texture (roof tiles, road markings, tree canopy) while the blur zones smooth everything into plastic-model smoothness. Cars become toy cars. People become figurines. Construction sites become Lego sets.
- **Mood:** Playful, god's-eye-view, detached amusement. The feeling of being a giant looking down at a tiny world. Charming and slightly surreal.
- **Reference points:** Vincent Laforet (aerial NYC), Olivo Barbieri, Ben Thomas, Keith Loutit (time-lapse miniatures), Wes Anderson set design

## FLUX keywords
