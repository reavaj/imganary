# Ukiyo-e

Ukiyo-e, meaning "pictures of the floating world," is a Japanese art movement that flourished from the 17th through 19th centuries, primarily during the Edo period. It captured the ephemeral beauty of everyday life and entertainment, focusing on courtesans, actors, landscapes, and folktales. Ukiyo-e prints are characterized by their woodblock printing technique and distinctive aesthetic qualities.

## Visual DNA
- **Lighting:** Flat, diffused lighting; minimal shadows, emphasizing form over realistic light play. Strong key light may be used to highlight specific areas.
- **Composition:** Often asymmetrical compositions; strong diagonal lines to create dynamism; use of cropping and unusual viewpoints inspired by Western art; hierarchical scaling; deliberate negative space.
- **Color palette:** Bold, flat, and often vibrant colors; use of key colors like vermilion red, indigo blue, and ochre yellow; limited gradation and blending.
- **Textures & materials:** Woodgrain visible in some prints; smooth, flat planes of color; use of bokashi (color gradation) for subtle effects; tactile quality arising from the woodblock process.
- **Mood:** Evocative, serene, and sometimes melancholic; captures a sense of fleeting beauty and pleasure; celebrates the transience of life.
- **Rendering:** Simplified forms and outlines; strong, clean linework; stylized representations of figures and landscapes; minimal perspective; attention to pattern and detail in clothing and decor.
