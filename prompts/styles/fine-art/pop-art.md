# Pop Art

The bold, mass-produced aesthetic of 1960s Pop Art — Warhol's screen prints, Lichtenstein's Ben-Day dots, the flat graphic punch of a silk-screened poster.

## Visual DNA
- **Lighting:** Completely flat and shadowless. Zero tonal variation within any single color area. No gradients, no shading, no light simulation whatsoever — light is a graphic design choice.
- **Composition:** Subject fills the entire frame, frontal or three-quarter view. Background is never empty — filled edge-to-edge with geometric patterns (circles, stripes, dots). Horror vacui — no blank space anywhere. Everything reads as a single flat plane, no depth or perspective.
- **Color palette:** Strictly limited primary colors — red, yellow, blue, black, white. No pastels, no secondary blends, no earth tones. Each color is a single flat unmodulated fill. Black carries the composition — heavy black outlines and black fill areas are the structural foundation.
- **Textures & materials:** Flat ink on paper. No texture within color fills — they are perfectly uniform. Background patterns are geometric and precise (concentric circles, parallel stripes, polka dots). The look of commercial silk-screen printing.
- **Mood:** Bold, graphic, immediate. The image hits like a poster — readable from across the room in one second.
- **Rendering:** Thick heavy-weight black ink outlines define every shape. Color fills inside outlines with absolutely no blending or gradation. Features simplified to their most graphic form — eyes are simple shapes, noses nearly absent, lips a single flat color shape. Completely 2D — figure and background on the same plane.
