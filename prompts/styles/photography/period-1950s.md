# 1950s Period Photography

Mid-century America frozen in silver gelatin — chrome diners, atomic-age optimism, Kodak box camera snapshots, and the birth of color photography.

## Visual DNA
- **Lighting:** Bright, direct flash (press photography style) or sunny-16 daylight. Hard shadows, high contrast. Color photography: slightly overexposed, Kodacolor warmth. Black and white: deep blacks, bright whites, full tonal range. Neon signs at dusk.
- **Composition:** Centered subjects, formal poses relaxing into candid. Cars as hero objects, photographed proudly in driveways. Diners, drive-ins, and gas stations as American icons. Families on front porches. The open road stretching to the horizon.
- **Color palette:** Early Kodacolor: pastel-shifted, slightly cyan shadows, warm highlights. Turquoise, pink, chrome silver, candy-apple red. Pastel suburban houses. Two-tone car paint. Black and white: silvery, clean, Ansel Adams contrast range.
- **Textures & materials:** Chrome everything — bumpers, jukeboxes, diner stools, toasters. Formica countertops. Vinyl booth seating. Tailfins and whitewall tires. Neon tube signage. Bakelite radios. Poodle skirts and saddle shoes. Crew cuts and pompadours. Linoleum floors.
- **Mood:** Optimistic, prosperous, atomic-age confidence. The American Dream at full beam. Clean, orderly, aspirational — but with an edge of conformity underneath.
- **Reference points:** Robert Frank (The Americans), Saul Leiter, Vivian Maier, LIFE Magazine, Edward Hopper (paintings as photographic reference), Slim Aarons
