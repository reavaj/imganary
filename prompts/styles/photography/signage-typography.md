# Signage & Typography

The visual culture of signs, lettering, and type in the built environment — hand-painted storefronts, neon marquees, vintage shop signs, chalkboard menus, highway billboards.

## Visual DNA
- **Lighting:** Natural daylight for painted signs. Neon signs self-illuminate against dusk or night sky. Interior light spilling through backlit sign letters. Warm tungsten from vintage bulb marquees. Mixed light — the sign competes with its environment.
- **Composition:** Sign as hero — filling the frame, shot straight-on or at dramatic angle. Environmental context (the building, the street, the neighborhood tells the story). Detail shots of letterform craftsmanship. Peeling paint and weathering as character.
- **Color palette:** Depends on era and context. Vintage: faded primaries, sun-bleached, oxidized metal. Neon: hot pink, electric blue, warm white against dark. Painted: bold primaries on white or brick. Chalkboard: white and warm earth tones on slate. Gold leaf on dark glass.
- **Textures & materials:** Hand-painted brushstrokes visible in lettering. Neon tube glass bends. Porcelain enamel on metal. Carved wood. Gold leaf on glass. Laser-cut acrylic. Chalk on slate. Vinyl on glass. LED channel letters. Rust bleeding through old paint.
- **Mood:** Nostalgic, urban, typographic craft. The personality of a place expressed through its signage. Every sign tells a story about who made it, when, and for whom.
- **Reference points:** Ed Ruscha (Twentysix Gasoline Stations), Walker Evans (vernacular signs), Las Vegas strip, Tokyo signage, Brooklyn hand-painted signs, English pub signs
