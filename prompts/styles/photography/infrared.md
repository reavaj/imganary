# Infrared Photography

The surreal inversion of infrared — white foliage, dark skies, glowing skin. The world seen in wavelengths beyond human vision.

## Visual DNA
- **Lighting:** Bright, direct sunlight works best (IR is strongest in direct sun). Hot spots on sun-facing surfaces. Deep shadows retain contrast. Backlighting through leaves creates intense white glow.
- **Composition:** Landscapes and nature scenes benefit most — the foliage inversion effect is the signature. Architecture against dark sky. Portraits gain an otherworldly quality (skin appears smooth and luminous, eyes darken). Water appears dark and mirror-like.
- **Color palette:** Classic IR is black and white with inverted tones: foliage goes bright white, sky goes near-black, skin glows porcelain. False-color IR adds surreal tints: pink/magenta foliage, cyan sky, golden highlights. Dark water, bright grass, inverted tonal relationships.
- **Textures & materials:** Foliage appears soft and cotton-like (the "Wood Effect"). Bark and stone retain texture. Skin smooths naturally. Atmospheric haze cuts through, making distant objects sharper than in visible light.
- **Mood:** Alien, dreamlike, otherworldly. Familiar landscapes made strange. The feeling of seeing a hidden world.
- **Reference points:** Richard Mosse (Infra, Enclave — Congo in pink IR), Kodak Aerochrome film, Hoya R72 filter photography, NASA false-color imaging
